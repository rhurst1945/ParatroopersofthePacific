Frequently Asked Questions

How many airborne units did the US Army send to fight in the Pacific during World War II?
Just two: the 503rd Regimental Combat Team (RCT), and the 11th Airborne Division. Compared to Europe, the topography of the Pacific offered fewer places to deploy paratroopers. But they were definitely used!
Besides the US Army, did paratroopers from other military branches or other countries fight in the Pacific?
Yes. The US Marine Corps sent their 1st Marine Parachute Regiment to the Pacific; however once there they fought as traditional Marines and were never deployed as paratroopers. In addition, the Japanese used paratroopers in the Pacific, seeing combat at Manado, Palembang, Koepang, Leyte, Burauen, and Okinawa.
Did the paratroopers ever fight as conventional infantry?
Yes. The 11th Airborne first saw combat on Leyte, coming ashore in landing craft like conventional infantry. The 503rd also saw combat on Mindoro as conventional infantry, and although a combat jump on Negros Island was planned, this was scrapped and they came ashore in the traditional (amphibious) manner.
Why are the Pacific Paratroopers largely overlooked today?
This is due to two factors. The first is that they are overshadowed by the exploits of their better-known comrades who fought in Europe, namely the 82nd Airborne and 101st Airborne Divisions. In addition to the 82nd and 101st, other US Army paratrooper units saw action in Europe. These battles were typically far bigger and involved far more paratroopers than those of the Pacific. The second factor is the US Marine Corps. When most people think of the War in the Pacific, the Marines are what usually come to mind, depsite the fact that the US Army had far more troops fighting in the Pacific than the Marines did.
